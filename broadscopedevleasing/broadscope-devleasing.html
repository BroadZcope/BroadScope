Article 01

Contract vs. Full-Time:
When to Hire What

The wrong hiring model costs more than a bad hire. A full-time developer in the wrong context bleeds salary with no output. A contractor in the wrong context creates dependency without ownership. Here's how to choose correctly — every time.

The decision most companies get wrong

Most companies default to full-time hiring because it feels more "committed." But commitment and output are not the same thing. Full-time hires are appropriate for specific conditions. Contractors are appropriate for different ones. Confusing them is expensive.

The question to ask is not "do we need a developer?" It's "what type of engagement does this work actually require?"

The core distinction: Full-time is for ongoing ownership. Contract is for defined delivery. If you can't define what "done" looks like, you're not ready to hire anyone.

When contract is the right call

  • 1
    Defined project with a clear endpoint. You need something built — a feature, an integration, a system. Once it's built, the need goes away or reduces significantly.
  • 2
    You need a specific skill you don't have internally. Your team is strong in backend but needs a frontend specialist for one build cycle. Hiring full-time for a temporary skill gap is almost always wrong.
  • 3
    You're pre-product-market-fit. You don't yet know what your engineering team needs to look like long-term. Committing to full-time salaries before you have clarity is a cash flow risk.
  • 4
    You need to move fast. Recruiting, interviewing, and onboarding a full-time hire takes 6–12 weeks. A vetted contractor can start in days.

When full-time is the right call

  • 1
    The work never ends. If the role involves continuous development, iteration, and system ownership with no defined endpoint — that's a full-time role.
  • 2
    Institutional knowledge matters. Some roles accumulate context that becomes irreplaceable. If losing the person means losing months of tacit knowledge, full-time creates the stability you need.
  • 3
    Team culture and collaboration are core to output. Contractors are optimized for delivery. Full-time hires are optimized for belonging. If your team's best work happens through long-term relationships and deep collaboration, invest in full-time.
  • 4
    You've validated the need and can afford the commitment. You have recurring revenue, clear product direction, and enough work to justify the cost. Now you're building infrastructure, not experimenting.

The hybrid approach most companies miss

The best engineering teams use contractors strategically to de-risk full-time decisions. A common pattern: hire a contractor first, assess their output and fit over 2–3 months, then convert to full-time if the work is ongoing and the person performs.

This approach validates both the role and the person before you commit to a full-time package. It protects your cash flow and your culture simultaneously.

How We Match Contract
Developers to Teams

Most contractor placements fail for one of three reasons: wrong skill match, wrong working style, or wrong expectations. Here's the process BroadScope uses to eliminate all three — so you get a developer who's productive from week one, not month three.

Why most contractor placements underdeliver

The standard placement model works like this: client shares a job description, agency sends CVs, client interviews, developer starts. The problem is that job descriptions describe technical skills. They don't describe working environment, codebase complexity, team communication style, or what "ownership" actually means in that company.

Developers who are excellent in one environment can be mediocre in another. Environment fit is as important as technical fit — and almost nobody screens for it.

Step 1 — We diagnose the role before matching

Before we suggest a single developer, we spend 30 minutes understanding four things: the technical scope (what are they building, what's the stack, what are the integration points), the team context (who are they working with, how does the team operate, what's the communication style), the definition of success (what does a great first month look like, what does done look like), and the failure modes (what went wrong with the last developer or hire in this role).

Most clients have never been asked that last question. The answers are usually illuminating.

What we're listening for: Not just "we need a React developer." We're listening for the unspoken requirements — the team's real working style, the actual complexity of the codebase, and whether the role needs someone who executes instructions or someone who owns outcomes.

Step 2 — We match on three dimensions, not one

  • 1
    Technical fit. Does the developer have direct, demonstrable experience with the specific technologies and problem types this role requires? Not adjacent experience — direct experience.
  • 2
    Working style fit. Does the developer work best with clear instructions and defined tasks, or do they thrive when given a problem and expected to own the solution? Misalignment here causes more friction than any technical skill gap.
  • 3
    Communication fit. Does the developer communicate proactively, flag blockers early, and update without being asked? In a distributed or fast-moving environment, communication style directly impacts output quality.

Step 3 — Onboarding that actually works

We don't hand over a developer and disappear. We facilitate a structured first week: a kickoff session where we align the developer and the client on scope, deliverables, and communication cadence; a codebase or project brief review; and a first-week check-in to surface any friction before it becomes a pattern.

The difference between a contractor who's productive in week one versus week six is almost entirely onboarding quality. We invest in this because it protects both the client and the developer.

What we guarantee (and what we don't)

We guarantee that the developer we place has been assessed against the three dimensions above and that they meet the technical requirements of the role. If within the first two weeks there is a clear mismatch, we address it immediately — either through coaching, scope adjustment, or replacement.

What we don't guarantee is that the developer will compensate for unclear scope, poor onboarding on the client side, or a dysfunctional team environment. The match works best when both sides show up prepared.
