Article 01

Website Redesign Checklist:
What Actually Matters

Most website redesigns are driven by one of two things: someone thinks it looks outdated, or a new marketing manager wants to put their stamp on things. Neither is a business case. Here's how to approach a redesign with the clarity that turns it into a genuine growth investment.

The question you need to answer first

Before you talk to a developer, before you collect inspiration screenshots, before you even decide you need a redesign — answer this: what is the single most important action you want a visitor to take on your website?

Book a call. Request a quote. Sign up for a trial. Buy a product. Everything on your website should be designed to lead visitors toward that action. If you don't know what that action is, any design you build will be aesthetically driven — and aesthetics don't convert.

The most common mistake: Building a website that communicates everything about the company and nothing about what the visitor should do next. A website is not a brochure. It is a conversion system.

Pre-redesign audit checklist

Before briefing any developer, work through these items. They will define what your redesign actually needs to achieve — and they'll save you from building the wrong thing beautifully.

  • 1
    Define your primary conversion goal. One action. Not three. What does a successful visit look like?
  • 2
    Audit your current conversion rate. How many visitors currently take the desired action? You need a baseline to know if the redesign worked.
  • 3
    Identify your three most important audience segments. Who visits your site? What do they need to know to take action? Are those three groups well-served by your current homepage?
  • 4
    Map the current user journey. From landing to conversion — what path does a visitor take? Where do they drop off? Analytics data makes this concrete.
  • 5
    Audit your current messaging. Does your homepage answer: What is this? Who is it for? Why should I care? What do I do next? If not, the redesign starts with messaging, not design.
  • 6
    Define technical requirements. Do you need a CMS? E-commerce? Booking integration? SEO infrastructure? These requirements drive platform decisions, and platform decisions made late are expensive.
  • 7
    Set a success metric. How will you measure whether the redesign achieved its goal? Conversion rate increase? More qualified leads? Reduced bounce rate? Define it before the build starts.

What to brief your developer on

A great developer brief contains: your primary conversion goal, your target audience segments and their core questions, the user journey you want to create, the technical integrations required, the content you have versus the content you need to create, your timeline, and your success metric.

What it should not contain: screenshots of competitor websites you like, vague adjectives like "modern" or "clean", and design opinions from people who aren't your target audience. Briefs based on taste produce websites that look good to the people who commissioned them. Briefs based on conversion goals produce websites that work.

The content problem most redesigns ignore

More redesigns stall on content than on design or development. Companies brief developers, approve wireframes, approve designs — and then discover they don't have the copy, images, or case studies needed to fill the pages. Content creation takes longer than people expect and requires decisions about positioning that are often unresolved.

Map your content requirements before the build starts. Identify what exists, what needs to be written, and who is responsible for creating it. Build content timelines into the project plan. Treat content as a dependency, not an afterthought.

Article 02

What Makes a High-Converting
Website (Beyond Design)

A beautiful website that doesn't convert is an expensive piece of digital furniture. Conversion is not a design outcome — it's an architecture outcome. Here's what actually drives visitors to take action, and how to build it intentionally into your site.

The three questions every visitor asks

When someone lands on your website — any page, from any source — they are subconsciously asking three questions. You have approximately 5 seconds to answer them before they leave.

  • 1
    "What is this?" Is it immediately clear what the company does and who it's for? Not clear after reading three paragraphs — clear within the first sentence of the headline.
  • 2
    "Why should I care?" Is the value proposition compelling to me, specifically? Does it speak to my problem, my industry, my situation — or does it sound like it could apply to any business in any sector?
  • 3
    "What do I do next?" Is there a clear, obvious next step? Is the call to action visible without scrolling? Is there one primary action, or are there five competing options that create decision paralysis?

These three questions are answered (or not) in the hero section of your homepage. Every conversion problem starts here.

The homepage test: Show your homepage to someone who has never heard of your company. Give them 5 seconds. Then ask: what does this company do? Who is it for? What are you supposed to do next? Their answers will tell you everything you need to know about your conversion problem.

Architecture decisions that drive conversion

ElementHigh-Converting ApproachCommon Mistake
HeadlineOutcome-focused, specific to audienceCompany name or vague tagline
CTA placementAbove the fold, repeated at logical scroll pointsOnly in footer or contact page
Social proofSpecific results, named clients, relevant industriesGeneric testimonials, unnamed sources
NavigationMinimal, guides toward conversionEverything accessible, no hierarchy
Page lengthLong enough to answer all objections, no longerEither too short (no trust) or too long (no direction)
Mobile experienceDesigned mobile-first, CTA always visibleDesktop design shrunk to mobile

How trust architecture works

Conversion is a trust transaction. A visitor doesn't convert because your website is beautiful — they convert because they trust that you can deliver what you're promising. Trust is built through specific, verifiable signals: named clients and case studies (not anonymous testimonials), specific outcomes with numbers (not vague claims like "we helped them grow"), visible team members and real credentials, and evidence that you understand their specific industry or problem.

Each section of a high-converting website is designed to answer the objection that's preventing the visitor from moving forward. The homepage builds initial trust. The services or solutions page resolves scope uncertainty. Case studies resolve performance uncertainty. The contact or booking page resolves commitment anxiety. Each section does a specific job in the conversion journey.

Performance and SEO as conversion factors

A beautiful website that loads slowly is a website that doesn't convert. Page speed directly affects bounce rate — and bounce rate directly affects conversion rate. The benchmark is a Largest Contentful Paint (LCP) under 2.5 seconds on mobile. Most websites don't meet this threshold. Most businesses don't know what their threshold is.

SEO and conversion are not competing priorities. A well-structured page optimised for a specific search intent will convert better than a generic page — because visitors arriving from specific searches have higher intent and find more relevant content. Structure your pages around the questions your target customers are actively searching, not around the way your internal team describes your services.

Measuring conversion accurately

You can't improve what you don't measure. Before launch, set up: Google Analytics 4 with conversion events configured, goal tracking for the primary conversion action, heatmaps (Hotjar or equivalent) to see where visitors click and where they stop scrolling, and session recording to observe real visitor behaviour. Review this data monthly, identify the biggest drop-off point in the conversion journey, and make one targeted change at a time. Iterative, data-driven improvement consistently outperforms periodic full redesigns.
